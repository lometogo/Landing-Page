How to Make Meetings Matter

Bold words are vocabulary targets — tap the superscript number to jump to the definition

Have you ever looked around a meeting at the number of attendees1 — or today's current Zoom video squares — and wondered: how much does this cost? There's a way to estimate2 that likely significant amount, thanks to this Meeting Calculator in Harvard Business Review.

While financial cost is one factor, what about the potential post meeting impact on productivity3, emotions or creativity? Liz Fosslien and Mollie West Duffy, the authors of "No Hard Feelings: The Secret Power of Embracing Emotions at Work" illustrated4 this "thank you" card to show the feelings many attendees may have experienced after a "bad" meeting.

When you multiply these costs times how many meetings per week, month or year, that's a lot of unproductive5 time. Research in Harvard Business Review summarizes that "on average, executives6 spend nearly 23 hours a week in them [meetings]." As this trend moves through the org chart7, how many hours of meetings do their direct reports8 attend and so forth?

Take a further look at this research of 182 senior managers in a range of industries:

"65% said meetings keep them from completing their own work. 71% said meetings are unproductive and inefficient. 64% said meetings come at the expense of deep thinking. 62% said meetings miss opportunities to bring the team closer together."

It's very clear that "not all meetings are created equal. Some meetings engage, inspire, and clarify, while others leave us confused, depleted43, and annoyed because we just lost two hours (or more) of our precious lives," according to research from the d School at Stanford.

Ok, enough meeting therapy, let's focus on change — how can we make meetings matter? There's been a lot of research, books with epic names — Death by Meeting — prestigious9 articles, blog posts and twitter threads published over the years. While there's not one solution to this question, I curated10 some of the top suggestions that I've learned along the way from inspiring sources and personal experiences to hopefully help make meetings matter:

1. Why Meet

The most important part of the meeting begins before it even takes place — to determine the reason for the meeting.

Atlassian, the makers of collaboration tools such as Trello, Confluence and Jira created this flow chart called "Why Do You Want To Call A Meeting," with candid11 recommendations of when to meet and when not to meet, you should instead:

"eat a donut, no meeting!" or "don't be lame, no meeting!"

To prepare for the meeting of your dreams, the d school at Stanford, led by Nadia Roumani the Senior Designer of the Designing for Social Systems (DSS) Program developed this design-thinking worksheet with great prompts12.

2. Establish Meeting Norms or "Zorms"

Now, in our extended digital state, it's important to establish modern meeting norms13, also known as "Zorms" since so many of us are using Zoom to meet. Stanford organizational behavior14 professor Bob Sutton coined15 this term in a Stanford Magazine article with some examples:

"Join meetings with video on and audio muted, use the hand-raise feature when you have a comment or question, introduce yourself before speaking, and use the mute button when you're not speaking."

While these might seem very kindergarten-esque16, aligning on how to meet can change the feeling of a meeting. Think about the way we prefer presentations or how we ask questions, all of this can set the right tone and feeling for meeting success. Asana, the project management tool company, established a "Meeting Manifesto17" with an illustrated guide for how to make meetings count.

3. Establish a Purpose

Scheduling18 start-up Calendly is built around helping people schedule meetings more efficiently19, saving you the back and forth of schedule tetris — when you share your availability, but the other person doesn't confirm fast enough, and you have to start from scratch.

Calendly offers these recommendations:

"If you aren't sure yet about the goal of the meeting or if you can accomplish the goal by any other means of communication, then cancel the meeting. If you have a one-sentence, outcome-driven purpose for each meeting that you hold, you'll see better results and be more efficient with your time."

McKinsey categorizes20 three types of meetings. It's important to distinguish21 the goal with each of them.

4. Invite the Right People and Give Them a Role

Who is in the room can make or break the meeting. Less is more, even the occasional "tourist" — the term McKinsey refers to guests — can be a distraction22. Business Insider shares a story about how Steve Jobs "ran meetings with a similar minimalism23 [to his designs]. He hated when they were too big because too many minds in a room got in the way of simplicity."

In order to minimize the attendance list, consider specific roles for participants. The suggested roles could follow this decision matrix defined by McKinsey or the system your organization uses.

Decision maker(s): Vote on action. They hold responsibility. There should be as few as possible.

Advisers: Assist with the decision making by giving input.

Recommenders: Manage the analyses25, explore the options, show the pros and cons, and ultimately recommend a course of action to the advisers and decision makers.

Execution partners: Get involved in implementing the decision. They ask clarifying26 questions.

There's much to be said for inviting people to join a meeting to learn more during their on-boarding27 or training. Don't discount the potential of additional voices who might observe and add value.

5. Organize the Meeting Around a Decision

In order for people to feel a sense of accomplishment28 from a meeting, it's important to write out the key decisions you want to make in a meeting.

This Harvard Business Review article references the style of Tom Wilson, the Chairman, President and CEO of The Allstate Corporation.

"He used to end each major meeting with a simple chart. For each significant29 decision there were three boxes: "Yes," "No," and "Defer." Under the latter, there was space to indicate the date to which the issue would be deferred30 and what additional actions or data were required to move to a "Yes" or "No" at that time. This helped drive clarity and closure and made his meetings more efficient and decisive."

Consider this decision chart for future meetings, even your one on one meetings. It could transform your feeling of productivity.

6. Set the Meeting Time

Calendars default to 30 minute increments31. Try making a meeting in less than 30 minutes. I like this idea of an 18 minute meeting, inspired by TED Talks. In Forbes Magazine, Carmine Gallo, the author of Talk Like TED, writes the following recommendations:

"TED talks are memorable because no speaker is allowed to talk for more than 18 minutes on the TED stage. It doesn't matter if your name is Bill Gates, Sheryl Sandberg, Bono, or Tony Robbins... TED curator46 Chris Anderson said it best, 'Eighteen minutes is long enough to be serious and short enough to hold people's attention.'"

He goes on to remind us about how much can be said in 18 minutes, citing how President John F. Kennedy inspired a nation to go to the moon in a 15-minute talk and how Dr. Martin Luther King, Jr. shared his "I have a dream" speech in 17 minutes.

While meetings aren't speeches, perhaps if we started thinking about them in this way, it could change their impact? If that method doesn't work, here's another chart of meeting times to consider.

7. Draft an Agenda

There's a lot to potentially talk about in a meeting. The more those topics are mapped out in advance, preferably in bullet points, the better.

Indeed offers several best practices when drafting an agenda:

"Some meeting agendas simply list a topic as a phrase, for example: 'rental equipment.' However, you can clarify each agenda item's purpose by phrasing discussion points as questions. For example, you could write, 'Under what conditions should we consider renting equipment instead of buying it?' These prompts can ensure you are inviting discussion and gathering all of the information you need for each agenda topic."

The science behind that approach is confirmed by research shared in Harvard Business Review by Steven G. Rogelberg, the Chancellor's Professor at the University of North Carolina Charlotte.

"Ultimately, a questions-based approach to agendas can bring focus, engagement44, and better performance to your meetings. If you have never tried this approach, give it a go. It's OK to experiment, reflect, learn, and tweak your approach. This work will not only help make meetings better, but will also build a broader team culture of learning, taking reasonable risks, and non-complacency45. And remember: if you can't think of any questions to be answered in a meeting, that may be your sign that a meeting is simply not needed. Give back the gift of time to would-be attendees. They will thank you."

Finally, I like Asana's Meeting Agenda Template, in particular, because it can help with tracking follow-up items. More on that topic in #11.

8. Share and Prepare in Advance

In addition to sharing the agenda, any materials should be shared before a meeting. Invite attendees to comment on them to increase engagement or ask them to bring one takeaway and start the meeting by inviting them to share.

For larger meetings with many speakers and a bigger attendance, I believe in drafting talking points to ensure that the speakers know the key messages they need to deliver.

In addition, if you are speaking during a meeting, prepare your thoughts in advance so that they can communicate the message you hope to convey.

9. Facilitate Well and Stay on Time

The facilitator32 can make or break the meeting. This person manages the direction of conversation, invites participation in an inclusive33 way and keeps everyone on time.

The conversation is guided by asking strategic questions based on the agenda, but then a facilitator must read the room to keep the meeting going in the right direction. If no one is talking, the meeting host invites participation. If someone is talking too much, the facilitator can graciously34 guide them in another direction. If you go off topic, the facilitator can bring you back.

Atlassian refers to facilitators as the builders of trust

"by asking questions that prompt a deeper discussion, even when you think you know the answer. Questions like 'Why do we think that's true?' or 'Can you expand on that?' or 'How could we measure that?' demonstrate humility35 and curiosity on your part, which sets the tone for the rest of the group."

In Slack's guide to effective meetings, they recommend:

"if you can have a designated facilitator to keep things humming along, even better. 'Smart facilitators are key to successful meetings,' says Jaffer. 'They keep people on track, are not afraid to interrupt, call out participants for being off-topic, and summarize the to-dos and next steps.'"

My secret to keeping meetings on time is a friendly Xylophone that I've had since I was six years old, but only recently started to use for meeting management. It makes a nice sound that reminds the presenters to move forward.

10. Give Attendees Roles

In addition to decision matrix36 roles, there are other ways to empower people in a meeting so they aren't tempted to check social media or multi-task on another project.

"thinking: note takers are the underestimated holders of power and team performance - they are the source of truth 24 hours later, and often the arbiters37 of our memory. am very grateful to my project management colleagues that move us forward." — Scott Belsky

If you need to incentivize38 note taking, take a look at this Scientific American:

"research which shows that writing notes by hand helps people learn more, recollect39 facts better later, and gain a deeper understanding of the material than when they type notes."

Other meeting attendees can have speaking roles and other ways to stay engaged in the conversation.

11. Assign Action Items and Deliverables

From my perspective, this is the most important part of meetings — what happens at the end.

In Slack's guide to effective meetings, they recommend:

"sum up the meeting with notes and action items. Make these notes accessible to everyone who attended the meeting. Consider sharing notes with others who couldn't attend. Or better yet, record meetings and send out a link to the audio or video afterward. This is particularly valuable for team members working remotely or companies with a globally distributed workforce40."

In addition, "assign action items or things to follow up on to specific individuals whenever possible. It's also helpful to schedule a deadline or a time when someone will check in on progress."

Conclusion

Now, all of this takes time to be done well. So, why not do a calendar audit41 to see what meetings you need and what meetings can be removed. From there, build in the time to prepare for meetings — start with 15 minutes one-two days before the meeting.

If you find yourself in a meeting that you haven't had time to prepare for, take a few minutes at the start of the meeting to map out the goals and questions, it won't be perfect, but it will be a step in the right direction.

The sign of a good meeting organizer is being open to feedback at any time. Don't over survey, but do invite direct or anonymous42 feedback to find out if meetings were meaningful, a good use of time or how they could be better.

Vocabulary Definitions

1. Attendees: (uh-TEN-deez) - people who are present at or taking part in an event or meeting; participants, guests, delegates. The attendees gathered in the conference room five minutes before the meeting began. ↑ back
2. Estimate: (ES-tuh-mut) - to roughly calculate or judge the value, number, or size of something; approximate, calculate, gauge. The manager tried to estimate how much the weekly team meetings were costing the company. ↑ back
3. Productivity: (pro-duk-TIV-ih-tee) - the efficiency with which a person or organization completes useful work; output, efficiency, performance. Poor meetings are one of the biggest threats to workplace productivity. ↑ back
4. Illustrated: (IL-uh-stray-ted) - created a picture, drawing, or visual example to explain or decorate something; depicted, visualized, portrayed. The authors illustrated their ideas with a humorous thank-you card showing common post-meeting emotions. ↑ back
5. Unproductive: (un-pruh-DUK-tiv) - failing to produce useful results or good use of time; ineffective, wasteful, fruitless. The survey showed that most senior managers considered their meetings unproductive. ↑ back
6. Executives: (eg-ZEK-yuh-tivz) - senior managers or leaders who hold decision-making authority in a company; directors, senior managers, leaders. Many executives spend nearly a full working day each week doing nothing but attending meetings. ↑ back
7. Org chart: (ORG chart) - short for "organizational chart"; a diagram showing the structure of a company and the relationships between different roles and departments; hierarchy diagram, company structure, organizational map. As you move down the org chart, the hours spent in meetings tend to multiply. ↑ back
8. Direct reports: (duh-REKT rih-PORTS) - employees who work directly under and report to a specific manager; subordinates, team members, reportees. A VP might spend 23 hours in meetings, but their direct reports may face an even heavier burden. ↑ back
9. Prestigious: (preh-STIJ-us) - having a high reputation and commanding respect; distinguished, respected, acclaimed. Articles in prestigious publications like Harvard Business Review carry significant weight in the business world. ↑ back
10. Curated: (KYOOR-ay-ted) - carefully selected and organized the best items from a larger collection; handpicked, compiled, selected. She curated the most practical advice from years of research and personal experience. ↑ back
11. Candid: (KAN-did) - honest and direct, even when the truth may be uncomfortable; frank, straightforward, blunt. The flowchart offered candid advice, including telling people to skip the meeting and eat a donut instead. ↑ back
12. Prompts: (PROMPTS) - questions or cues designed to encourage thinking or a specific response; cues, questions, triggers. The design-thinking worksheet contained prompts that helped teams plan meetings more intentionally. ↑ back
13. Norms: (NORMZ) - accepted standards or rules of behavior within a group or society; standards, conventions, expectations. Establishing clear norms for meetings helps everyone know what is expected of them. ↑ back
14. Organizational behavior: (or-guh-nih-ZAY-shuh-nul bih-HAY-vyer) - the academic study of how people and groups act within organizations; workplace psychology, management science, human behavior at work. Professors of organizational behavior study how team dynamics, leadership, and culture shape workplace outcomes. ↑ back
15. Coined: (KOYND) - invented or introduced a new word or phrase; created, invented, originated. Professor Bob Sutton coined the term "Zorms" to describe meeting norms for Zoom calls. ↑ back
16. Kindergarten-esque: (kin-der-GAR-ten-ESK) - resembling something from kindergarten; overly basic or childlike in nature; elementary, rudimentary, basic. Some of the meeting etiquette rules seemed kindergarten-esque, yet they were surprisingly necessary in the remote work era. ↑ back
17. Manifesto: (man-ih-FES-toh) - a public declaration of beliefs, values, or intentions, often used to guide behavior; declaration, mission statement, charter. Asana published a Meeting Manifesto to help teams commit to running better, more purposeful meetings. ↑ back
18. Scheduling: (SKEJ-yoo-ling) - the process of arranging and planning times for events or tasks; planning, organizing, booking. Scheduling meetings across multiple time zones can quickly become a logistical nightmare. ↑ back
19. Efficiently: (ih-FISH-unt-lee) - in a way that achieves maximum results with minimum wasted time or effort; effectively, productively, smoothly. Calendly helps people schedule meetings more efficiently by eliminating back-and-forth emails. ↑ back
20. Categorizes: (KAT-uh-guh-ryz-ez) - sorts or groups things into specific categories or types; classifies, groups, organizes. McKinsey categorizes meetings into three distinct types, each with a different purpose and goal. ↑ back
21. Distinguish: (dih-STING-gwish) - to recognize or identify the differences between things; differentiate, tell apart, separate. It's important to distinguish between a meeting meant to share information and one meant to make a decision. ↑ back
22. Distraction: (dih-STRAK-shun) - something that takes attention away from what someone is trying to focus on; interruption, diversion, interference. Even one unnecessary attendee can be a distraction that slows down the decision-making process. ↑ back
23. Minimalism: (MIN-ih-muh-liz-um) - a style or approach that uses only the most essential elements and removes everything unnecessary; simplicity, restraint, spareness. Steve Jobs applied his design philosophy of minimalism to meetings, keeping them small and focused. ↑ back
25. Analyses: (uh-NAL-ih-seez) - plural of "analysis"; detailed examinations of data or situations to understand them better; evaluations, assessments, studies. Recommenders are responsible for managing the analyses that lead to a final decision. ↑ back
26. Clarifying: (KLAIR-ih-fy-ing) - making something easier to understand by removing confusion or ambiguity; explaining, elaborating, clearing up. Execution partners attend meetings primarily to ask clarifying questions about how decisions will be implemented. ↑ back
27. On-boarding: (ON-bor-ding) - the process of integrating and training new employees into an organization; orientation, induction, training. Inviting new hires to sit in on meetings is a common on-boarding strategy to help them learn the culture. ↑ back
28. Accomplishment: (uh-KOM-plish-ment) - something successfully completed or achieved; achievement, success, result. People leave better meetings with a sense of accomplishment because clear decisions were made. ↑ back
29. Significant: (sig-NIF-ih-kunt) - important, large, or meaningful enough to deserve attention; important, major, notable. Each significant decision in the meeting was assigned to one of three columns: Yes, No, or Defer. ↑ back
30. Deferred: (dih-FERD) - postponed or delayed to a later time; postponed, delayed, put off. Any issue that couldn't be resolved in the meeting was deferred, with a specific date set for revisiting it. ↑ back
31. Increments: (IN-kruh-ments) - fixed, regular amounts by which something increases or is divided; intervals, units, steps. Most calendar apps default to 30-minute increments, which often leads to unnecessarily long meetings. ↑ back
32. Facilitator: (fuh-SIL-ih-tay-ter) - a person who guides a group discussion or process to keep it on track and productive; moderator, discussion leader, mediator. A skilled facilitator knows when to step in, when to stay quiet, and how to draw out quieter voices. ↑ back
33. Inclusive: (in-KLOO-siv) - making sure all people are welcomed, heard, and able to participate; welcoming, open, participatory. A good facilitator invites participation in an inclusive way so that no one dominates the conversation. ↑ back
34. Graciously: (GRAY-shus-lee) - in a polite, kind, and tactful manner; tactfully, diplomatically, courteously. When someone talks too long, a good facilitator graciously redirects the conversation without causing offense. ↑ back
35. Humility: (hyoo-MIL-ih-tee) - the quality of not thinking you are better than others; being open to learning and admitting what you don't know; modesty, openness, self-awareness. Asking "why do we think that's true?" demonstrates intellectual humility and invites deeper group thinking. ↑ back
36. Decision matrix: (dih-SIH-zhun MAY-triks) - a structured framework that assigns specific roles and responsibilities to participants in a decision-making process; role framework, responsibility chart, RACI model. McKinsey's decision matrix helps teams clarify who decides, who advises, and who executes. ↑ back
37. Arbiters: (AR-bih-terz) - people who have the authority or power to judge or settle a matter; judges, authorities, decision-makers. Note-takers become the arbiters of collective memory because they hold the written record of what was agreed. ↑ back
38. Incentivize: (in-SEN-tih-vyz) - to motivate or encourage someone to do something by offering a reward or benefit; motivate, encourage, reward. Managers can incentivize note-taking by publicly recognizing the people who do it well. ↑ back
39. Recollect: (rek-uh-LEKT) - to remember or recall something from the past; recall, remember, retrieve. Research shows that people who take handwritten notes recollect information more accurately than those who type. ↑ back
40. Distributed workforce: (dis-TRIB-yoo-ted WERK-fors) - employees who work in different physical locations rather than all being in one central office; remote team, dispersed team, virtual workforce. Recording meetings and sharing links is especially valuable for companies with a distributed workforce across multiple time zones. ↑ back
41. Audit: (AW-dit) - a systematic review or examination of something to assess its quality or identify problems; review, assessment, examination. The author suggests doing a calendar audit to identify which recurring meetings are actually necessary. ↑ back
42. Anonymous: (uh-NON-ih-mus) - without revealing one's name or identity; unnamed, unidentified, confidential. Inviting anonymous feedback helps employees share honest opinions without fear of judgment. ↑ back
43. Depleted: (dih-PLEE-ted) - drained of energy, strength, or resources; exhausted, drained, worn out. After a series of pointless meetings, even motivated employees can feel completely depleted. ↑ back
44. Engagement: (en-GAYJ-ment) - the level of interest, attention, and active involvement a person brings to a task or situation; involvement, participation, interest. Framing agenda items as questions dramatically increases attendee engagement during meetings. ↑ back
45. Complacency: (kum-PLAY-sun-see) - a feeling of satisfied self-satisfaction that prevents one from noticing problems or trying to improve; self-satisfaction, smugness, indifference. A question-driven meeting culture actively fights complacency by keeping teams curious and engaged. ↑ back
46. Curator: (kyoo-RAY-ter) - a person who selects, organizes, and presents content or a collection with expert judgment; editor, selector, organizer. TED's curator sets the 18-minute rule because he understands the science of human attention spans. ↑ back
