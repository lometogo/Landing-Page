I don't want a pickle …
The Royal Enfield Meteor 350 comes roaring into my life!

A personal essay by Paul Erlandson

@hotrodanglican  ·  MAR 25, 2026

As Arlo Guthrie famously sang:

I don't want a pickle
Just want to ride on my motorsickle
And I don't want a tickle
'Cause I'd rather ride on my motorsickle
And I don't want to die
I just want to ride on my motorcy...cle

After 35 years (almost to the week) of not owning or riding a motorcycle, I will soon be taking deliver11 of a new 2024 Royal Enfield Meteor 350. I can't wait!

When I first realized that I was going to get a motorcycle-sized tax refund10 from the IRS, I did some searching on old 1960s chopper-style3 bikes. But I know how that would have gone. I'd never have gotten the thing running properly, and it would just have been a beautiful, un-rideable4 sculpture12 sitting in my son's barn.

Author’s Note

Wanna feel young forever? Hop your aged ass on a growly single cylinder Euro-designed, Indo-manufactured Meteor 350. Works everywhere.

Coincidentally1, we have at least five motorcycle riders in our small Anglican parish2. Two of them ride Royal Enfield bikes. One had a 650 cc version and the other fellow a Meteor 350, the same model I just bought. Not only did both of these gentlemen have high praises for the Royal Enfield brand, but I found about a dozen YouTube reviews of the bike with nothing bad to say about the Meteor 350.

I mean, except that it is under-powered7. At a whopping13 20.2 horsepower8, it takes over 17 seconds to reach 60 mph. Top speed is 72 mph, so I won't be doing any freeway cruising9. But for what it is, it seems to be excellent, and an optimal5 choice for someone new to riding or returning after a decades-long absence6. Here is one sample video. I could easily find 10 others.

Vocabulary Definitions

1 Coincidentally: (koh-IN-sih-DEN-tlee) — happening at the same time or in the same way by chance, without being planned; by chance, as it happens, as luck would have it. Coincidentally, both women had chosen the same dress for the party without telling each other. ↑ back
2 Anglican parish: (ANG-gli-kun PAIR-ish) — a local community of the Anglican Church (the worldwide church that grew out of the Church of England), and the geographic area it serves; congregation, church community, local diocese. The Anglican parish organized a soup kitchen that served over two hundred meals every Sunday. ↑ back
3 Chopper-style: (CHOP-ur STY-ul) — referring to a type of custom motorcycle popular in the 1960s and 1970s, typically with a low seat, long front forks, and a stripped-down or heavily modified look; custom, old-school, retro-style. The mechanic spent two years building a chopper-style bike with extended handlebars and a flame paint job. ↑ back
4 Un-rideable: (un-RY-duh-bul) — not able to be ridden, either because it is broken, unsafe, or not in working condition; non-functional, inoperable, out of commission. After the crash, the bicycle's frame was bent and completely un-rideable. ↑ back
5 Optimal: (OP-tih-mul) — the best or most favorable choice given the situation or conditions; ideal, perfect, most suitable. Morning is the optimal time to water the garden because the sun is not yet too hot. ↑ back
6 Decades-long absence: (deh-KAYDZ-lawng AB-sents) — a period of being away from something or not doing something for twenty years or more; long gap, extended break, prolonged time away. After a decades-long absence from the stage, the actress returned to Broadway to a standing ovation. ↑ back
7 Under-powered: (UN-dur-POW-urd) — having less engine power than is needed or expected for a given task or comparison; weak, low-powered, lacking punch. The small engine felt under-powered when the truck had to climb a steep mountain road. ↑ back
8 Horsepower: (HORS-pow-ur) — a unit used to measure the power output of an engine; originally based on the work a horse could do, now a standard way to compare engine strength; engine output, power rating, hp. The sports car produced over 400 horsepower, allowing it to go from zero to sixty miles per hour in under four seconds. ↑ back
9 Freeway cruising: (FREE-way KROOZ-ing) — driving or riding at highway speeds on a high-speed road with no traffic lights or intersections, typically 65 mph or above; highway driving, interstate travel, open-road riding. With a full tank of gas and an open road, she spent the afternoon freeway cruising all the way to the coast. ↑ back
10 Tax refund: (TAKS REE-fund) — money returned to a taxpayer by the government when they have paid more in taxes during the year than they actually owed; rebate, reimbursement, money back. She used her tax refund to pay off a credit card bill and put the rest into savings. ↑ back
11 Deliver (take delivery of): (deh-LIV-ur) — in this context, to officially receive a purchased item, especially a vehicle, from the seller; receive, pick up, take possession of. They drove two hours to the dealership to take delivery of their new car. ↑ back
12 Sculpture: (SKULP-chur) — a three-dimensional work of art created by shaping or assembling materials; here used figuratively to mean something that looks impressive but just sits there without functioning; statue, art piece, decorative object. The abandoned car in the driveway had become more of a sculpture than a vehicle — beautiful but going nowhere. ↑ back
13 Whopping: (WOP-ing) — used to emphasize that something is surprisingly large or impressive in amount — often used humorously when the number is actually quite small; massive, enormous, staggering. He ate a whopping five slices of pizza before anyone else had finished their first. ↑ back
