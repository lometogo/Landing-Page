Iran War: How Did We Get Here?

Author unknown  ·  June 2026
America — and its case for war — is a loser

A war started to change the Iranian regime1 — or help the Iranian people, or conclusively2 deny Iran a nuclear weapon, or take out all of its missile/drone capabilities — has ended with the U.S. signing a ceasefire3 framework4 that achieved none of its original goals.

MAGA is a loser

Trump's war of choice led to higher inflation5, the president's worst-ever approval rating, and a lot of pissed off right-wing politicians and commentators, including Tucker Carlson, the top-rated news podcaster, who now says he will be leaving the Republican Party.

White House unity is a loser

It's as if this war was started and ended by two entirely different administrations. A war that started with Rubio making the US sound like a classic, George W. Bush-era neocon6 state ends with the Jacksonian7 isolationist8 JD Vance negotiating and explaining the framework with values opposite9 to those that justified10 the war, in the first place.

Iran is a loser

The deal framework looks good for Iran, but the national economy is a mess, and the state of military and civilian infrastructure11 is pitiful12.

Israel is a loser

After unsuccessfully petitioning13 every administration since the 1990s for help bombing Iran, Benjamin Netanyahu finally got his wish from Trump … and his hand-picked war has ended with a surrender14 memo so disastrous15 that Israeli media is accusing Trump of abandoning Israel to its ultimate enemy.

Disarmament is a loser

Trump claimed in February that this war was about taking a super-weapon16 out of Iran's hands (nuclear weapons), but the war ironically17 gave Iran a new super-weapon, which is its ability to shut down the Strait of Hormuz or make money off it, like a tolled18 highway.

📖 Vocabulary

1Regime (ruh-ZHEEM) - a government, especially an authoritarian one, or the system by which a country is governed; government, administration, rule, leadership. Western governments imposed sanctions in an attempt to pressure the regime into abandoning its nuclear program. ↑ back
2Conclusively (kun-KLOO-siv-lee) - in a way that settles a matter definitively, leaving no room for doubt or further argument; definitively, decisively, finally, irrefutably. The investigation failed to conclusively establish who had authorized the attack. ↑ back
3Ceasefire (SEES-fyr) - a temporary or permanent agreement between warring parties to stop fighting; truce, armistice, halt, suspension of hostilities. Both sides agreed to a ceasefire, but analysts warned it was fragile and could collapse within weeks. ↑ back
4Framework (FRAYM-wurk) - a basic structure or set of principles that underlies a system, agreement, or plan, within which more detailed arrangements can be developed; structure, outline, blueprint, foundation. The two countries signed a framework agreement, leaving the finer details to be negotiated over the following months. ↑ back
5Inflation (in-FLAY-shun) - the rate at which the general level of prices for goods and services rises, reducing purchasing power; price rise, cost increase, monetary expansion. Surging energy prices drove inflation to its highest level in forty years, squeezing household budgets across the country. ↑ back
6Neocon (NEE-oh-kon) - short for neoconservative; a political position advocating for the aggressive promotion of democracy and U.S. interests abroad, often through military intervention; neoconservative, interventionist, hawkish. The foreign policy speech drew immediate criticism from those who saw it as a return to the neocon playbook of the early 2000s. ↑ back
7Jacksonian (jak-SOH-nee-un) - relating to the foreign policy tradition associated with Andrew Jackson, emphasizing national sovereignty, military strength used only to protect direct U.S. interests, and deep skepticism of international entanglements; nationalist, sovereigntist, America-first. His Jacksonian instincts made him deeply reluctant to commit troops to a conflict that posed no direct threat to American soil. ↑ back
8Isolationist (eye-suh-LAY-shun-ist) - a person or policy that favors withdrawing from international affairs and avoiding foreign alliances or conflicts; non-interventionist, inward-looking, nationalist, withdrawalist. The isolationist wing of the party argued that the country had no business spending billions on wars ten thousand miles from home. ↑ back
9Opposite (OP-uh-zit) - here used to mean directly contradictory or incompatible; contradictory, conflicting, contrary, antithetical. The two men held opposite views on almost every major foreign policy question, yet were somehow serving in the same cabinet. ↑ back
10Justified (JUS-tih-fyd) - shown or claimed to be right, reasonable, or necessary; warranted, legitimized, rationalized, defended. The administration justified the military action on the grounds of an imminent threat, a claim that later proved difficult to substantiate. ↑ back
11Infrastructure (IN-fruh-struk-chur) - the fundamental physical and organizational systems of a country or organization, including roads, power grids, water supply, and communications networks; systems, facilities, foundations, framework. Years of sanctions and conflict had left the country's infrastructure in a state of severe disrepair. ↑ back
12Pitiful (PIT-ih-ful) - deserving or arousing pity because of being inadequate, wretched, or in a very poor state; wretched, deplorable, woeful, inadequate. The pitiful state of the country's hospitals shocked even the most experienced aid workers. ↑ back
13Petitioning (puh-TISH-un-ing) - formally requesting something from an authority or powerful party, especially repeatedly or over a long period; appealing, lobbying, requesting, imploring. The smaller nations had spent years petitioning the Security Council for intervention, with little result. ↑ back
14Surrender (suh-REN-dur) - the act of giving up or yielding to an opponent, or in a broader sense, capitulating on one's stated goals or principles; capitulation, concession, submission, climbdown. Critics described the agreement not as a negotiated peace but as a thinly disguised surrender. ↑ back
15Disastrous (dih-ZAS-trus) - causing great harm, damage, or failure; extremely bad in its outcome; catastrophic, calamitous, devastating, ruinous. The disastrous miscalculation cost the general his career and the country its most strategic ally in the region. ↑ back
16Super-weapon (SOO-pur-wep-un) - a weapon or capability so powerful or strategically significant that it fundamentally shifts the balance of power; ultimate weapon, decisive capability, game-changer, strategic asset. Control of the strait gave the country a super-weapon it had never possessed before — the ability to strangle global oil supplies at will. ↑ back
17Ironically (eye-RON-ik-lee) - in a way that is contrary to what was expected or intended, often with a sense of bitter or amusing contradiction; paradoxically, perversely, contrary to expectations. Ironically, the sanctions designed to weaken the government only strengthened its grip on the population by creating a common enemy. ↑ back
18Tolled (TOHLD) - subject to a fee charged for the use of a road, bridge, or waterway; here used figuratively to describe Iran's ability to extract payment or concessions from ships using the strait; fee-based, charged, levied. The newly tolled crossing generated hundreds of millions in revenue annually, transforming what had once been a public right-of-way into a profit center. ↑ back
